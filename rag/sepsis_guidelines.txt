## Surviving Sepsis Campaign: International Guidelines for Management of Sepsis and Septic Shock 2021
## Source: Evans et al., Critical Care Medicine, 2021;49(11):e1063-e1143
## Published by: Society of Critical Care Medicine and European Society of Intensive Care Medicine

### Screening and Early Recognition

Recommendation: Use a performance improvement programme for sepsis, including sepsis screening for acutely ill, high-risk patients.
Strength: Strong recommendation | Evidence: Moderate
Meta-analysis showed 34% mortality reduction with sepsis screening programs (OR 0.66; 95% CI 0.61-0.72).

Recommendation: Do NOT use qSOFA compared to SIRS, NEWS, or MEWS as a single screening tool for sepsis.
Strength: Strong recommendation | Evidence: Moderate
qSOFA is more specific but less sensitive than SIRS for early identification. It provides prognostic but not diagnostic value.

Recommendation: Measure blood lactate in patients with suspected sepsis.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Low
Lactate sensitivities 66-83%; specificities 80-85%. Elevated lactate (>2 mmol/L) is a key marker of tissue hypoperfusion.

### Initial Resuscitation

Recommendation: Sepsis and septic shock are medical emergencies. Treatment and resuscitation should begin immediately.
Strength: Best Practice Statement

Recommendation: Administer at least 30 mL/kg of IV crystalloid fluid within the first 3 hours of resuscitation for sepsis-induced hypotension or septic shock.
Strength: Weak recommendation (downgraded from strong in 2016) | Evidence: Low
Based on observational evidence. Limited prospective trial data.

Recommendation: Use dynamic measures to guide fluid resuscitation, over physical examination or static parameters alone.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Very Low
Dynamic assessment associated with reduced mortality (RR 0.59; 95% CI 0.42-0.83) and shorter ICU stay.

Recommendation: Guide resuscitation to decrease serum lactate in patients with elevated lactate levels.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Low
Must interpret lactate within clinical context. Normal lactate not achievable in all shock patients.

Recommendation: Use capillary refill time as an adjunct to other perfusion measures to guide resuscitation.
Strength: Weak recommendation (NEW in 2021) | Evidence: Low
ANDROMEDA-SHOCK study showed reduced SOFA scores but no significant mortality difference.

### MAP Target

Recommendation: Target an initial mean arterial pressure (MAP) of 65 mmHg over higher MAP targets in septic shock patients requiring vasopressors.
Strength: Strong recommendation | Evidence: Moderate
RCT showed no mortality benefit at higher MAP targets. Higher MAP targets associated with increased atrial fibrillation. Permissive hypotension (MAP 60-65 mmHg) may be safe in patients aged 65 and older.

### ICU Admission

Recommendation: Admit sepsis and septic shock patients to the ICU within 6 hours.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Low
ED-to-ICU delays >6 hours associated with 17% vs 12.9% mortality.

### Antimicrobial Therapy

Recommendation: Continuously re-evaluate and search for alternative diagnoses. Discontinue empiric antimicrobials if an alternative non-infectious cause is demonstrated.
Strength: Best Practice Statement
One-third of sepsis diagnoses turn out to be non-infectious conditions.

Recommendation: For septic shock, administer antimicrobials immediately, ideally within 1 hour of recognition.
Strength: Strong recommendation | Evidence: Low
Each additional hour delay associated with 1.04-1.16 increased odds of mortality in observational studies.

Recommendation: For sepsis without shock, rapidly assess likelihood of infectious versus non-infectious causes within 3 hours.
Strength: Best Practice Statement

Recommendation: For possible sepsis without shock, administer antimicrobials within 3 hours if concern for infection persists.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Very Low

Recommendation: For low likelihood of infection without shock, defer antimicrobials while continuing close monitoring.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Very Low

Recommendation: Do NOT use procalcitonin plus clinical evaluation to decide when to start antimicrobials, over clinical evaluation alone.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Very Low
Meta-analysis of 3 RCTs (n=1,769) showed no mortality difference (RR 0.99; 95% CI 0.86-1.15).

### Empiric Antimicrobial Selection

Recommendation: Use empiric antimicrobials with MRSA coverage in patients at high risk for MRSA.
Strength: Best Practice Statement
Risk factors: prior MRSA infection/colonization, recent IV antibiotics, recurrent skin infections, invasive devices, hemodialysis, recent hospitalization.

Recommendation: Do NOT use empiric antimicrobials with MRSA coverage in patients at low risk for MRSA.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Low
Unnecessary MRSA coverage associated with higher mortality in some studies.

Recommendation: Use two antimicrobials with gram-negative coverage for empiric treatment in patients at high risk for multi-drug resistant organisms.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Very Low
Risk factors: prior resistant organism infection/colonization, hospital-acquired infection, recent broad-spectrum antibiotics.

Recommendation: Do NOT use double gram-negative coverage once causative pathogen and susceptibilities are known.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Very Low
Meta-analysis of 10 RCTs showed no mortality difference between mono vs combination therapy.

### Antifungal Therapy

Recommendation: Use empiric antifungal therapy in patients at high risk for invasive fungal infection.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Low
Risk factors: febrile neutropenia failing antibiotics, Candida colonization at multiple sites, severe immunosuppression, GI perforation, TPN, severe thermal injury.

Recommendation: Do NOT use empiric antifungal therapy in patients at low risk for fungal infection.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Low

### Antibiotic Delivery

Recommendation: Use prolonged infusion of beta-lactams for maintenance (after an initial bolus) over conventional bolus infusion in sepsis/septic shock.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Moderate
Meta-analyses showed reduced mortality (RR 0.70; 95% CI 0.57-0.87). Must administer loading dose before prolonged infusion.

Recommendation: Optimize dosing strategies based on accepted pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic (PK/PD) principles and specific drug properties.
Strength: Best Practice Statement
Requires consideration of kidney function, body weight, renal replacement therapy, and extracorporeal support.

### Source Control

Recommendation: Rapidly identify or exclude a specific anatomical diagnosis of infection that requires emergent source control. Implement source control as soon as medically and logistically practical.
Strength: Best Practice Statement
Target: 6-12 hours after diagnosis. Amenable sources: intra-abdominal abscess, GI perforation, cholangitis, cholecystitis, pyelonephritis with obstruction, necrotizing soft tissue infection, device-related infections. Use least invasive effective option.

Recommendation: Promptly remove intravascular access devices that are a possible source of sepsis after other vascular access has been established.
Strength: Best Practice Statement

### De-escalation and Duration

Recommendation: Perform daily assessment for de-escalation of antimicrobials over fixed durations without daily reassessment.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Very Low
Meta-analysis of 13 studies: improved mortality with de-escalation (RR 0.72; 95% CI 0.57-0.91). Associated with shorter hospital stay (MD -5.56 days; 95% CI -7.68 to -3.44).

Recommendation: Use shorter over longer courses of antimicrobial therapy in sepsis patients with adequate source control and clinical improvement.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Low

### Vasopressor Therapy

Recommendation: Norepinephrine is the first-line vasopressor for septic shock (target MAP >= 65 mmHg).
Strength: Strong recommendation | Evidence: Moderate

Recommendation: Start vasopressors peripherally if central venous access is not yet available, rather than delaying initiation.
Strength: Weak recommendation (NEW in 2021)
Safe for short-term use (<6 hours) when infused through proximal vein. Enables faster BP goal achievement.

Recommendation: Add vasopressin (up to 0.03 U/min) as second-line agent if MAP target not achieved with norepinephrine alone.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Moderate

Recommendation: Add epinephrine as third-line vasopressor if needed.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Low

Recommendation: Use dobutamine for patients with persistent hypoperfusion despite adequate fluid loading and vasopressor use (cardiac dysfunction).
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Low

Recommendation: Do NOT use dopamine except in highly selected patients (e.g., low risk of tachyarrhythmias with absolute or relative bradycardia).
Strength: Strong recommendation | Evidence: High

### Fluid Types

Recommendation: Use crystalloids as the first-line fluid for resuscitation.
Strength: Strong recommendation

Recommendation: Use balanced crystalloids (e.g., Lactated Ringer's, Plasma-Lyte) over normal saline (0.9% NaCl).
Strength: Weak recommendation (NEW in 2021)
SMART trial showed lower 30-day mortality in sepsis subgroup with balanced fluids (OR 0.90).

Recommendation: Use albumin in addition to crystalloids when patients require substantial volumes of crystalloids.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Low

Recommendation: Do NOT use hydroxyethyl starches (HES) for volume replacement.
Strength: Strong recommendation | Evidence: High
Associated with increased mortality and acute kidney injury.

Recommendation: Do NOT use gelatins for volume replacement.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Low
Inconclusive mortality effects, increased adverse effects, higher costs.

### Corticosteroids

Recommendation: Use IV corticosteroids (e.g., hydrocortisone 200 mg/day) for septic shock patients with ongoing vasopressor requirement.
Strength: Weak recommendation (CHANGED from 2016 recommendation against use)
Multiple RCTs showed shortened shock duration. Mortality effects unclear. Desirable effects of shock resolution outweigh potential harms.

Recommendation: Do NOT use corticosteroids for sepsis without shock.
Strength: Weak recommendation

### Vitamin C

Recommendation: Do NOT use IV vitamin C for sepsis or septic shock.
Strength: Weak recommendation (NEW in 2021)
Meta-analysis found no benefit.

### Blood Products

Recommendation: Transfuse red blood cells only when hemoglobin concentration decreases to <7.0 g/dL in adults, in the absence of extenuating circumstances (myocardial ischemia, severe hypoxemia, acute hemorrhage).
Strength: Strong recommendation | Evidence: Moderate

Recommendation: Do NOT use erythropoietin for treatment of sepsis-related anemia.
Strength: Strong recommendation

Recommendation: Do NOT use fresh frozen plasma to correct clotting abnormalities in the absence of bleeding or planned invasive procedures.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Very Low

### Mechanical Ventilation (Sepsis-Induced ARDS)

Recommendation: Use low tidal volume ventilation (6 mL/kg predicted body weight) for sepsis-induced ARDS.
Strength: Strong recommendation | Evidence: High

Recommendation: Target plateau pressure <30 cm H2O.
Strength: Strong recommendation | Evidence: Moderate

Recommendation: Use higher PEEP over lower PEEP for moderate-to-severe ARDS (PaO2/FiO2 <200).
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Moderate

Recommendation: Use prone positioning for >12 hours/day in severe ARDS (PaO2/FiO2 <150).
Strength: Strong recommendation | Evidence: Moderate

Recommendation: Do NOT routinely use recruitment maneuvers.
Strength: Strong recommendation against | Evidence: Moderate

Recommendation: Use neuromuscular blocking agents (NMBA) for up to 48 hours in early severe ARDS if sedation alone insufficient.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Low

### Glucose Control

Recommendation: Commence insulin dosing when two consecutive blood glucose levels are >180 mg/dL (10 mmol/L).
Strength: Strong recommendation

Recommendation: Target blood glucose <=180 mg/dL (10 mmol/L) rather than <=110 mg/dL (6.1 mmol/L).
Strength: Strong recommendation | Evidence: High
Tight glucose control associated with increased hypoglycemia without mortality benefit.

Recommendation: Monitor blood glucose every 1-2 hours until stable, then every 4 hours in patients receiving insulin infusions.
Strength: Best Practice Statement

### Renal Replacement Therapy

Recommendation: Use either continuous or intermittent renal replacement therapy in patients with sepsis and acute kidney injury.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Low

Recommendation: Use continuous therapies to facilitate management of fluid balance in hemodynamically unstable patients.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Very Low

### VTE Prophylaxis

Recommendation: Use pharmacologic prophylaxis (unfractionated heparin or low-molecular-weight heparin) against venous thromboembolism (VTE) unless contraindicated.
Strength: Strong recommendation | Evidence: Moderate

Recommendation: Use low-molecular-weight heparin over unfractionated heparin for VTE prophylaxis when possible.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Low

### Stress Ulcer Prophylaxis

Recommendation: Give stress ulcer prophylaxis to sepsis patients with risk factors for GI bleeding.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Low
Use proton pump inhibitors or H2 receptor antagonists.

### Nutrition

Recommendation: Initiate early enteral nutrition (within 72 hours) rather than parenteral nutrition alone or no nutrition.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Very Low

Recommendation: Do NOT use mandatory full caloric feeding in the first week of sepsis. Advance as tolerated.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Moderate

### Goals of Care and Long-term Outcomes (NEW in 2021)

Recommendation: Discuss goals of care and prognosis with patients and families within 72 hours of ICU admission.
Strength: Best Practice Statement

Recommendation: Address goals of care early, not solely at end-of-life.
Strength: Best Practice Statement

Recommendation: Screen critically ill sepsis patients for economic and social support needs and make referrals as available.
Strength: Best Practice Statement

Recommendation: Assess patients for physical, cognitive, and emotional problems before hospital discharge and at follow-up.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Very Low

Recommendation: Provide information on sepsis, its sequelae, and recovery expectations at hospital discharge and at follow-up.
Strength: Weak recommendation | Evidence: Very Low


## 2023 Update: Emergency Department Management of Sepsis and Septic Shock
## Source: Guarino et al., Journal of Clinical Medicine, 2023;12(9):3188

### Emergency Department Antimicrobial Protocols

Recommendation: Obtain microbiological samples as soon as possible on ED admission before starting antibiotics.
Recommendation: Administer empiric broad-spectrum antimicrobials within 1 hour for suspected sepsis with or without shock.
Recommendation: Administer within 3 hours for low-to-moderate risk patients without shock.
Recommendation: Use multidrug regimens for septic shock covering MRSA, ESBL, Pseudomonas, and Candida based on local resistance patterns and patient risk factors.
Recommendation: De-escalate antimicrobials daily based on culture results and clinical response.
Recommendation: Procalcitonin (PCT) is less effective than clinical judgment alone for initiation decisions but may guide treatment duration.
Recommendation: Presepsin (PSP) offers higher diagnostic accuracy than PCT but has limited availability.

### Emergency Department Fluid Management (2023 Update)

Recommendation: Use balanced crystalloids as the fluid of choice for sepsis resuscitation.
Recommendation: Avoid colloids due to increased risk of acute kidney injury (AKI).
Recommendation: Albumin provides no mortality benefit over crystalloids.
Recommendation: Use individualized fluid strategy based on fluid tolerance and fluid responsiveness.
Recommendation: Administer small, repeated boluses (250-500 mL) with continuous hemodynamic monitoring rather than large fixed volumes.
Recommendation: Avoid fluid overload as excessive volumes damage the endothelial glycocalyx.
Recommendation: Use dynamic assessment tools: passive leg raise, stroke volume variation, IVC collapsibility index.
Recommendation: Static measures (heart rate, central venous pressure, systolic blood pressure) are poor indicators of fluid status.

### Emergency Department Vasopressor Protocols (2023 Update)

Recommendation: Norepinephrine remains first-line vasopressor. Dosing: 0.1-1.2 mcg/kg/min. Target MAP >= 65 mmHg.
Recommendation: Early norepinephrine administration prevents fluid overload and reduces mortality.
Recommendation: Add vasopressin (0.25-0.5 mcg/kg/min) as second-line if MAP target not achieved with norepinephrine.
Recommendation: Vasopressin may reduce need for renal replacement therapy but does not decrease mortality independently.
Recommendation: Reserve epinephrine for refractory shock despite norepinephrine + vasopressin. Greater beta-adrenergic effects; higher risk of tachycardia and arrhythmia.
Recommendation: Permissive hypotension (MAP 60-65 mmHg) is acceptable in patients aged 65 and older. Higher targets do not provide additional benefit.

### Emergency Department Oxygenation Protocols

Recommendation: Start supplemental oxygen at 15 L/min via reservoir mask for acute sepsis.
Recommendation: Target SpO2 94-98% (or 88-92% if risk of hypercapnia, e.g., COPD).
Recommendation: High-flow nasal cannula (HFNC) is recommended for hypoxic respiratory failure over non-invasive ventilation.
Recommendation: HFNC provides benefits during weaning and preventing reintubation but requires close monitoring.
Recommendation: Low tidal volume ventilation (6 mL/kg predicted body weight) is preferred over 10 mL/kg.

### Additional ED Interventions (2023 Update)

Recommendation: Use LMWH over unfractionated heparin for VTE prophylaxis. Mechanical prophylaxis if anticoagulation is contraindicated.
Recommendation: Target glucose 144-180 mg/dL using insulin infusion. Avoid oral hypoglycemic agents in sepsis.
Recommendation: Consider sodium bicarbonate for severe metabolic acidosis (pH < 7.1, bicarbonate < 5 mEq/L) and patients with AKI stage 2-3.
Recommendation: Acetaminophen for symptomatic fever only — no outcome improvement demonstrated.
Recommendation: Renal replacement therapy is not indicated by sepsis alone. Follow AKI-specific guidelines.

### Sepsis Diagnostic Criteria (Current)

Sepsis is defined as life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by dysregulated host response to infection: SOFA score >= 2 with suspected or confirmed infection.
Septic Shock is defined as sepsis requiring vasopressor therapy to maintain MAP >= 65 mmHg AND serum lactate >= 2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation.
Screening: NEWS or SIRS criteria preferred over qSOFA for emergency department screening. qSOFA has low sensitivity and should not be used as a single screening tool.
Machine learning algorithms for sepsis identification achieve average 81% sensitivity and 72% specificity.

## AAFP Practice Guidelines: Surviving Sepsis Updated Recommendations 2022
## Source: American Academy of Family Physicians, 2022

### AAFP Key Recommendations

Recommendation: Machine learning algorithms are the most accurate way to identify sepsis, with an average 81% sensitivity and 72% specificity.
Recommendation: Resuscitation with 30 mL/kg of crystalloid fluids is recommended within the first 3 hours. Prefer balanced crystalloids (Lactated Ringer's) over normal saline.
Recommendation: Norepinephrine is the first-line vasopressor. Start peripherally rather than delaying for central venous access.
Recommendation: Consider adding vasopressin at 0.03 units/minute if norepinephrine requirements escalate.
Recommendation: For patients in septic shock, antimicrobials should be started within 1 hour.
Recommendation: In sepsis without shock, antimicrobials are recommended within 3 hours.
Recommendation: Use prolonged IV infusions rather than intermittent boluses for beta-lactam antibiotics.
Recommendation: High-flow nasal oxygen is recommended over non-invasive ventilation for hypoxic respiratory failure in sepsis.
Recommendation: Prone positioning for more than 12 hours per day within the first 36 hours improves survival in severe ARDS.
Recommendation: IV corticosteroids accelerate resolution of shock by 1.5 days. Typical dosing: hydrocortisone 200 mg daily after 4 hours of vasopressor therapy.
Recommendation: Restrictive transfusion strategy is recommended (hemoglobin threshold < 7 g/dL).
Recommendation: Source control (drainage, debridement, device removal) is essential after initial resuscitation and should not be delayed.
